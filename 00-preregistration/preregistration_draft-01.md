## Hypothesis



### Hypothesis 1 (Normality)
We hypothesise that both statistical and prescriptive norms influence causal selection, and listeners make inferences about the speakers' beliefs regarding norms based on the listeners’ knowledge of causal structure.

- Hyp. 1a: Speakers attribute causality to a cause that they view as prescriptively normal in a situation where the causal structure is disjunctive, and prescriptively abnormal when the causal structure is conjunctive. (Exp1)
- Hyp. 1b: Speakers attribute causality to a cause that they view as statistically normal in a situation where the causal structure is disjunctive, and statistically abnormal when the causal structure is conjunctive. (Exp2)
- Hyp. 1c: Listeners infer that a speaker believes their selected cause to be prescriptively normal in a situation where the causal structure is disjunctive, and prescriptively abnormal when the causal structure is conjunctive. (Exp1)
- Hyp. 1d: Listeners infer that a speaker believes their selected cause to be statistically normal in a situation where the causal structure is disjunctive, and statistically abnormal when the causal structure is conjunctive. (Exp2)

### Hypothesis 2 (Interaction of normalities)

We hypothesise that the effects of statistical and prescriptive norms combine to influence causal selection, and listener's inferences about speaker's beliefs regarding prescriptive norms based on the listener's knowledge of causal structure are affected by their knowledge about the speaker's beliefs regarding statistical norms.

- Hyp. 2a: Speakers attribute more causality when they view a cause as both prescriptively and statistically normal in a disjunctive causal structure, and as abnormal in a conjunctive structure, than when they view it as normal in only one respect.
- Hyp. 2b: Listeners infer that a speaker believes their selected cause to be prescriptively *more* normal when the speaker also believes that cause to be statistically normal, rather than abnormal.

Both tested in Exp3.
### Hypothesis 3 (Outcome Valence)
We hypothesise that outcome valence influences causal selection, and listeners make inferences about the speaker's beliefs regarding prescriptive normality based on the listener's knowledge of the outcome valence.

- Hyp. 3a (categorical): Speakers' causal attribution changes when the outcome valence is non-neutral than when it is neutral. That is, the following inequality holds: $|r_{\text{neutral}} - r_{\text{pleasant/unpleasant}}| > 1$.
- Hyp. 3b (gradient-positive): Speakers attribute greater causality to a cause they believe to be normal when the outcome is pleasant than when it is neutral.
- Hyp. 3c (gradient-negative): Speakers attribute less causality to a cause they believe to be normal when the outcome is unpleasant than when it is neutral.

($r$ is causal attribution rating in the speaker task.)

All tested in Exp1, Exp2, and Exp3.
